Drag the Smith chart or tune R and X — see Γ on the chart and, beside it, what that means for a coax line and an antenna.

Core idea: mismatch creates reflection. On a line that becomes a standing wave; at an antenna it means less power radiated — so a distant receiver hears a weaker signal.
What am I looking at?

The Smith chart is the complex plane of Γ = Γr + jΓi. Every passive load with R ≥ 0 lands inside (or on) the unit circle. Drag the orange marker (or use the inputs) to change the TX antenna load Z. That decides how much power is radiated into free space. The second antenna does not change with your drag — it only captures the field that was radiated, then feeds the RX radio.

  • Center — matched TX load, Γ = 0 → max radiation → strong induced voltage at the RX antenna.
  • Right rim — open circuit (Γ ≈ +1) → almost nothing radiates → RX silent.
  • Left rim — short circuit (Γ ≈ −1) → same: reflection, not radiation.
  • |Γ| — larger radius ⇒ more reflection at your antenna ⇒ weaker waves for the second antenna.
  • Path distance — free-space falloff ~1/d²; even a good match fades if the RX antenna is far.

Formulas used here

z = ZL / Z₀
Γ = (z − 1) / (z + 1)
ZL = Z₀ (1 + Γ) / (1 − Γ)
VSWR = (1 + |Γ|) / (1 − |Γ|)
RL = −20 log₁₀(|Γ|) dB
ML = −10 log₁₀(1 − |Γ|²) dB
Relative RX level ∝ (1 − |Γ|²) / d²
